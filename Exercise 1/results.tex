\chapter{Results}
\section{Uncertainty}
In order to compare our measurements of the lasers intensity to the Fresnel equation prediction, we need to determine the refractive index.
This is determinable using Snell's law, and the fact that air has refractive index approximately equal to 1.
To get a good estimate however, we first need to find the errors of measurement. 
To find the error of our measurement of the refractive index we calculate the propagation of uncertainty.
In the case of refraction from glass to air $n = \frac{\sin(\theta_2)}{\sin(\theta_1)} = \frac{\sin(\theta_d + \theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_1)}$ and therefore
\begin{align*}
    \sigma_n &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial n}{\partial \theta_1} \sigma_{\theta_1} \right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial n}{\partial \theta_d} \sigma_{\theta_d} \right)^2} \\
    % &=  \left(\left( \frac{\cos(\theta_d + \theta_1)\sin(\theta_1) - \cos(\theta_1)\sin(\theta_d + \theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_1)^2} \sigma_{\theta_1} \right)^2 + \left(\frac{\cos(\theta_d + \theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_1)} \sigma_{\theta_d} \right)^2\right)^{1/2} \\
    &= \left(\left( \frac{\cos(\theta_2)\sin(\theta_1) - \cos(\theta_1)\sin(\theta_2)}{\sin(\theta_1)^2} \sigma_{\theta_1} \right)^2\right.\\
    &\quad + \left.\left(\frac{\cos(\theta_2)}{\sin(\theta_1)} \sigma_{\theta_d} \right)^2\right)^{1/2} \\
\intertext{from air to glass $n = \frac{\sin(\theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_2)} = \frac{\sin(\theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_1 - \theta_d)}$ so}
    \sigma_n &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial n}{\partial \theta_1} \sigma_{\theta_1} \right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial n}{\partial \theta_d} \sigma_{\theta_d} \right)^2} \\
    % &=  \sqrt{\left( \frac{\cos(\theta_1)\sin(\theta_d + \theta_1) - \cos(\theta_d + \theta_1)\sin(\theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_d + \theta_1)^2} \sigma_{\theta_1} \right)^2 + \left(\frac{\sin(\theta_1)\cos(\theta_d + \theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_d + \theta_1)^2} \sigma_{\theta_d} \right)^2} \\
    &= \left(\left( \frac{\cos(\theta_1)\sin(\theta_2) - \cos(\theta_2)\sin(\theta_1)}{\sin(\theta_2)^2} \sigma_{\theta_1} \right)^2\right.\\
    &\quad + \left.\left(\frac{\sin(\theta_1)\cos(\theta_2)}{\sin(\theta_2)^2} \sigma_{\theta_d} \right)^2\right)^{1/2}. \\
\end{align*}

\subsection{The refractive index}
If we use these errors to calculate a weighted mean using the following formula
\[n_{\textup{mean}} = \frac{\sum_i \frac{n_i}{\sigma_{n_i}}}{\sum_i \sigma_{n_i}}, \quad \sigma_{n_\text{mean}}^2 = \frac{1}{\sum_i \sigma_{n_i}^{-2}}\]
and plot it against $\theta_1$ we get figure \ref{fig:weighted_average}.
   \begin{figure*}[h]
        \includesvg[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/Weighted Average.svg}
        \centering
        \caption{Estimation of the refractive index of the semicircular prism used in the experiment}
        \label{fig:weighted_average}
    \end{figure*}
It is to be noted, that we under the experiment noticed widening of the laser when it hit the detector at large angles of incidence. Widening of the laser is some sort of optical aberation that depends on the angles of incidence, which meant that we were not able to measure the entire laser beam, and also that the uncertainty of our angle measurements increased with the angle of incidence.
We have not taken this into consideration in the data analysis, and have instead run with an error $\sigma_\theta = 0.5$, since the scale had 1 degree increments. 
If we attempt to include a primitive model of how this uncertainty increases with angle, using $\sigma_\theta = 0.5 + \frac{\theta_1}{\max(\theta_1)}$, we instead get figure \ref{fig:new_weighted_average}.
   \begin{figure*}[h]
        \includesvg[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/Weighted AverageV2.svg}
        \centering
        \caption{Estimation of the refractive index of the semicircular prism used in the experiment, using a linearly increasing model for the error}
        \label{fig:new_weighted_average}
    \end{figure*}
As can be seem from the figures, in each case we get quite different indices of refraction for the 2 different experiments.
We will therefore be using the index of refraction measured in one experiment (as in air to glass or glass to air), in the Fresnel equations for the same experiment, as we do not know which is more accurate.

\subsection{The Fresnel equation}
If we subtract the background intensity (measured once at the beginning of the experiment for both s and p polarisation) from all the data points $V'_i$, for $i = R,T$ such that $V_i = V'_i - V_{\text{background}}$ and we then use the intensity measured without the glass (also measured independently for s and p polarisation) $V_{0} = V_{0}' - V_{\text{background}}$, as the baseline for $R$ and $T$, i.e $R = \frac{V_R}{V_0}$ and $T = \frac{V_T}{V_0}$, we get a really poor agreement between data and the expected values shown in \ref{fig:bad_fresnel_comparison}, probably because we aren't catching all of the light as mentioned earlier.
   \begin{figure*}[h]
        \includesvg[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/GlassToAirIntensityBack.svg}
        \includesvg[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/AirToGlassIntensityBack.svg}
        \centering
        \caption{Fresnel equation expectations compared to measurements, using the measured $V_0$ as baseline}
        \label{fig:bad_fresnel_comparison}
    \end{figure*}
What works way better is using $R = \frac{V_R}{V_R + V_T}$ and $T = \frac{V_T}{V_R + V_T}$, that is, since $V_R$ and $V_T$ together isn't the total intensity from the laser, we instead assume that $V_T = \alpha \tilde{V_T}$ and $V_R = \alpha \tilde{V_R}$ meaning that they are somehow reduced by a constant factor $\alpha(\theta_1)$ from what they are supposed to be, because then the ratios $R$ and $T$ would be unchanged.
These new definitions make for the following standard deviations, and figure \ref{fig:fresnell_comparison}. 
\begin{align*}
\sigma_R &= \sigma_T\\ 
&= \sqrt{\left( \frac{V_R}{V_R + V_T} \sigma_{V_T} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{V_T}{V_R + V_T} \sigma_{V_R} \right)^2}\\
&= \sqrt{\frac{V_T^2\sigma_{V_R}^2 + V_R^2\sigma_{V_T}^2 }{(V_R + V_T)^2}}
\end{align*}
Here $\sigma_{V_T}$ and $\sigma_{V_R}$ is the uncertainty in the measurement from the light sensor. They are really small, and therefore the resulting uncertainty in $R$ and $T$ also become really small.

        \includesvg[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/GlassToAirIntensity.svg}
        \includesvg[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/AirToGlassIntensity.svg}
        \centering
        \caption{Fresnel equation expectations compared to measurements, using $V_0 = V_R + V_T$}
        \label{fig:fresnell_comparison}
    \end{figure*}\\
A different approach was also tried for for a sanity check which should have resulted in a refractive index close to those reached with the weighted mean.
As earlier an reflective index of air being equal to one and with Snell's Law the Fresnel Equations can be rewritten as only being dependent on the angle of incidence $\theta_{\textup{i}}$ and the refractive index of the second media $n_{\textup{2}}$. 
As can be seen in the following equations where $\Phi(\theta_{\textup{i}}, n_{\textup{1}}, n_{\textup{2}}) = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{n_{\textup{1}}}{n_{\textup{2}}} \cdot \sin\theta_i\right) \\ \approx \Phi (\theta_{\textup{i}}, n_{\textup{2}}) = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{n_{\textup{2}}} \cdot \sin\theta_i\right)$
\begin{align*}
            R_{\textup{p}}(\theta_{\textup{i}}, n_{\textup{2}}) &= \frac{\tan^{2}(\theta_i - \Phi)}{\tan^{2}(\theta_i + \Phi)}\\
            R_{\textup{s}}(\theta_{\textup{i}}, n_{\textup{2}}) &= \frac{\sin^{2}(\theta_i - \Phi)}{\sin^{2}(\theta_i + \Phi)}\\
            T_{\textup{p}}(\theta_{\textup{i}}, n_{\textup{2}}) &= \frac{\sin{2\theta_i}\sin{2\Phi}}{\sin^{2}(\theta_i + \Phi)\cos^{2}(\theta_i - \Phi)}\\
            T_{\textup{s}}(\theta_{\textup{i}}, n_{\textup{2}}) &= \frac{\sin{2\theta_i}\sin{2\Phi}}{\sin^{2}(\theta_i + \Phi)}.\\
\end{align*}
Using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm through \textit{scipy}'s \textit{curve\_fit} function. It was possible to fit a function over a set of fixed set of incidence angles and optimizing for the best fitting $n_{\textup{2}}$ the following indices for each set seen in Table 1 was achived.
